Basics

This page helps you decide what to protect, from whom, and what “good enough” looks like. We focus on reducing exposure and hardening the accounts attackers most often target.

A quick threat model

You don’t need paranoia; you need clarity. Answer these questions:

  • What are you protecting? (accounts, finances, physical location, family)
  • Who might target you? (ex-partner, scammer, stalker, random attacker)
  • How could they get leverage? (password reuse, public records, social posts)
  • What would “harm” look like? (account takeover, identity theft, threats, job impact)

OSINT in one paragraph

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) is information about you that’s publicly available or easily obtained: old usernames, photos, relatives, addresses, employer info, school affiliations, court records, and more. The goal is to reduce what’s public and make your accounts hard to take over.

Accounts: the big 4 controls

1) Unique passwords

Password reuse turns one leak into many account takeovers. Use a password manager and change the “important” accounts first: email, Apple/Google, bank, social.

2) MFA (2FA)

Prefer an authenticator app or a security key. SMS is better than nothing, but it’s weaker.

3) Recovery security

Attackers often use password resets. Secure your email inbox, set strong recovery options, and remove old phone numbers and emails.

4) Updates

Keep your phone and laptop updated. Most real-world attacks succeed because devices are unpatched.

Phishing: what to look for (and what to do)

Most account takeovers start with social engineering: an email, text, phone call, or DM that pressures you into clicking a link or sharing a code.

Common red flags

  • Urgency: “act now,” “your account will be closed.”
  • Unexpected MFA codes: you get a code you didn’t request.
  • Link mismatches: the visible link text doesn’t match where it goes.
  • Payment pressure: gift cards, crypto, wire transfers.

Safer response

  • Don’t click the link or open attachments.
  • Verify by navigating to the official site/app yourself.
  • Lock down the account: change password + enable MFA.
  • Report scams using official channels (see Resources).

Oversharing traps (common in small towns)

  • Routine posts: “Every Friday at…” can reveal patterns.
  • School + sport + schedule: kids’ safety can depend on limiting public details.
  • Local groups: neighborhood pages often expose addresses and family relationships.
  • Photos: street signs, house numbers, work badges, and license plates are “free OSINT.”
